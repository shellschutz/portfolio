Defragmenting (2025 - Ongoing)

The outside is being ravaged by a mysterious downpour of a glass, you are forced to take shelter in a seemingly innocuous rundown hotel. Yet, stuck with you are people who you wish you would never see again. What will you do to survive?

Defragmenting mixes elements of visual novels and point-and-click adventure games to tell a story of desperation, shared tragedy and a deep rooted desire to overcome barriers of ourselves in the face of an impending doom.

Art Style

My main role for this project is 2D artist, my first official role as one since leaving the mobile game industry to work on our imagined indie game.

The art style was a stepped up from our team's previous project, Fragile, which was much more monotone and minimalist. For this project, i put in an effort to improve my own art style, trying replicate a comic book/graphic novel style, while trying to synthesize my own style. It results in a more detailed and stylized comic book style. My direct inspirations were: Scarlet Hollow and Disco Elysium.

Image: Game Main UI

The art style is a result of trial and error, in the end it feels very original as we opted to take risk in making it as visually striking as possible. My requirement for this project was for us to have an Overlay blend mode effect on top of the entire game, in which I would add a canvas overlay to add texture to the art style.

What's a visual novel without some background scenery? My biggest contribution for this game by far is the vast amount of scene art for each area of the game.

Sound Design

For sound design, I had three principals that I had applied for this project:

  • First is that sound playing are meant to reflects what's happening on screen, they are feedback mechanism in the purest form.
  • Second is that sounds should be approximated, priority isn't completely realism but representation and feeling.
  • Third is that sounds follows the rules of space, sound plays in different space have different effects to them.

So the first principal: sounds should reflects what's happening on screen is pretty straight forward. For example, in the game sometimes you have different way of transit through area, when you walk, you hear footsteps, when you drive, you hear the car engine, etc. That much will help you instantly get immersed because you wont' be wondering why it's making footstep sounds when you're in a car.

Image: Park Scene

Sound: Footstep

Image: Mall Drive Scene

Sound: Car Drive

However, sometimes things are a bit harder to reflect as sound effects, which poses as problems for sound design: One is that the sound is made by an unrealistic/fantastical concept, such as a glass rain happening outside. Two is that there's a budget limitation.

The second principal: sounds should be approximated, helps with both of the afore mentioned problems.

Let's take the first problem of the unrealistic concept of a glass rain. We have to break the concept down into some core ideas:

  • It's a rain, which is a large quantity of something, which often are water droplets, falling from the sky in a manner that is continuous.
  • The something here is actually glass, a solid but fragile material that breaks upon high impact.

With that I can start to approximate the sound: First thing that I thought of that would sounds similar to raining glass is the sound of a hailstorm, raining ice, a solid material raining from the sky.

Sound: Hailstorm Ambience

The hailstorm already sounds pretty good as an approximation but it's not complete. When glass break, it makes a pretty distinct crash sound that most people are familiar with, which this doesn't have yet. Therefore, I mixed various sounds of glass breaking and rattling, trying to blend it as so that it doesn't cut through the ambience.

Sound: Glass Break Sounds

When you mixed those together, you get an approximated representation of what a glass rain sounds like:

Track: Mixed Glass Rain Ambience

Image: Glass Rain Ambience Audio Effects

I also added some effects on to the sound so that it feels more natural and less harsh. Mainly filters that cut out unneeded frequencies, but also a delay effect to make the rain feel more full, and a tape simulator to have a subtle grainy analog feel.

Now, we're going back and dealing with the second issue: budget limitation, being the most prevalent. Most sounds that we want couldn't be synthesized or recorded professionally due to the lack of props. While most sounds can be sourced from Freesound.org or various asset packs, some just have a much more specific requirement to be sourced. For us, this was the issue with Character Voice Acting

There are 5 main characters that's needed to be represented audibly, each with different emotions and responses. Without voice talents, we could only source voice sfx packs online, but the voice lines are generic and awkward to use on its own. So instead, I cut the sliced the voice lines up, sometimes stitching them together to sound like an expression. For example, below are a character's scoffing sounds:

Image: Annoyed Grace

Sound: Grace Annoyed Scoff

Sound: Grace Neutral Scoff

The scoff is a short huff of air, commonly to denote annoyance, which perfectly fits Grace's character as a closed off person who hates social interaction. So in order to create Grace's "voice", I sliced a bunch of sounds from our voice packs and put them together to sounds like a scoff. These are the steps:

  • Take a laughing/giggling sound, preferably breathy.
  • Take the first start of the laughter, the huff of air being exhale. It should sounds sorta punchy.
  • Take the end of the laughter, or a part that sounds breathy, like they are letting the rest of the breath out or their breathe escaping.
  • Combine the first part and the second part with some adjustment, then you will get your short huff of air with the rest of the air

Image: Anatomy of Grace's Annoyed Scoff

This process was a lot of experimentation with slicing sounds and stitching them together, which was only to approximate the expression of the characters to a believable degree. Of course not every natural sounds can be created by slicing, so things like laughter and questioning were sliced straight from the voice pack with only minor processing done to them. Overall, that's how we implemented the voices of characters in our game without having to spend time and budget for professional voice acting. These are the voice packs I had used:

  • Super Dialogue Audio Pack V1 produced by Dillon Becker
  • Female Voice Clips by Rookie
  • Generic Male Voice Pack by Dan Bosley @daniconshow on X

The final principal I had was: sounds should follow the rule of space. The most prevalent example of this is again the glass rain: it is more muffled as you goes deeper inside of the hotel. This was done in Unity's Audio Mixer by a tweaking exposed mixer's parameters, linked to effects.

Image: All of the exposed params

This was with help from our programmer that we have a custom Audio Param Preset system, used to change the value of effects whenever called. The muffling of the glass rain in the hotel is done by calling these presets to toggle a low pass filter, making the rain sound muffled.

Image: Preset for when the hotel door is open.

Image: Preset for when the hotel door is closed.

The spatial effects helped immersing the player into the atmosphere, by directly playing into their sense of surrounding. It's what makes the ambience feels like an actual object and not just background noise.

Overall, this is the project that I really poured my hardest into sound design, making it as immersive as I can. Part of this is because the sound design of our last project, Fragile, was judged by officials as "muted", so this time I left no stone unturned, and prove to myself that I can create an atmosphere using sounds alone.
